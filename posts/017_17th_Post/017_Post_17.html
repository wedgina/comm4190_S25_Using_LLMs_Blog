Flashbulb memories are memories people have regarding how they found out about an event, particularly a shocking one. For those who were alive then, many remember where they were for 9/11 and exactly how they found out about it. These memories tend to evoke quite strong memories and emotions. Due to the passion and strength behind recalling these emotions, many often feel that this is a memory they are able to recall to near perfect. This is not necessarily the case, and actually quite the opposite many times. There is no guarantee to accuracy but it is still interesting how much people are able to collectively recall a memory about an event so vividly. In my blog, I will be using examples of common flashbulb memories and asking Claude AI to generate a possible and believable flashbulb memory as well.

Memory 1 : 9/11

Code 
def generate_memory(prompt, model='claude-3-5-haiku-20241022', max_tokens=1000, temperature=0.2):
    message = client.messages.create(
        model=model,
        max_tokens=max_tokens,
        temperature=temperature,
        messages=[
            {"role": "user", "content": prompt}
        ]
    )
    return message.content[0].text
prompt = "Generate a memory as if someone was remembering exactly where they were, how they found out, and what they were feeling when 9/11 happened"
response_911 = generate_memory(prompt)
print(response_911)

Around 9:15 AM, he turned up the volume, and we all watched in stunned silence as the news replayed the footage of the planes hitting the World Trade Center. I remember feeling this strange mix of disbelief and horror. The towers were so close - we could see the New York City skyline from our school windows, and many of my classmates' parents worked in Manhattan.

My heart was racing. My dad worked in Tower 2, and for what felt like endless hours, I couldn't reach him on the phone. The cell networks were jammed, and every minute felt like an eternity. Some of my classmates were crying, others were just frozen in shock. The world seemed to stop that morning.

When I finally heard my dad's voice that afternoon - he had evacuated safely - I broke down in tears. But so many others weren't as lucky. The fear, the uncertainty, the collective grief - it was unlike anything I'd ever experienced. That day changed everything, not just for me, but for an entire generation.

Memory 2 : Covid Lockdown

My Memory

I was still in high school. In the previous week leading up to the announcement, my econ teacher kept insisting they were never going to cancel school, whenever he was constantly being asked. Students were getting paranoid, as I remember when it was time to wheel out the chromebooks in class, classmates expeditiously went for wipes to wipe down their computers because they were growing worrisome about germs. I attended school that day and went to my previous school with my friend, where we volunteered at with the school band. My dad picked me up that day and gave my friend a ride back to her house and dropped me off at home. I also remember it was almost 6 and I was sitting on the couch when I got the email from my school saying they are going to cancel school for the next two weeks. I had believed we were going to come back at the time (we never did), but I was happy to finally have a break.

I was sitting in my small studio apartment in downtown Chicago, wearing my favorite worn-out gray sweatpants and an old college t-shirt. It was March 13th, 2020, and I was working from my makeshift dining table desk, my laptop humming softly. The afternoon sunlight was filtering through the half-closed blinds, casting long shadows across the hardwood floor.

My phone buzzed with a news alert, and I remember feeling a strange mix of anxiety and disbelief as I read the headline: "Nationwide Lockdown Announced Due to COVID-19 Pandemic." My hands were suddenly cold, and I could feel my heart racing. I turned on the TV, and the governor was speaking, his voice serious and measured, explaining the stay-at-home order.

The initial feeling was surreal - like I was watching a movie instead of experiencing real life. Shock mixed with a deep, gnawing fear. I thought about my parents, who were in the high-risk age group, and my mind started spinning with worst-case scenarios. Tears welled up unexpectedly, not from sadness exactly, but from the overwhelming uncertainty of it all.

I called my mom, my voice shaking slightly, trying to sound calm. "We'll be okay," I remember saying, more to reassure myself than her. Outside, the usually bustling street was becoming eerily quiet, with fewer cars and people visible.

The world had changed in an instant, and I knew nothing would be the same again.

What I think about Claude

Here's a memory of Kobe Bryant's death:

I was sitting in my kitchen, nursing a cup of coffee on that cold Sunday morning in January, scrolling mindlessly through my phone. It was January 26th, 2020. The sunlight was just starting to filter through the window when a breaking news alert suddenly popped up on my screen. At first, I didn't process the words - something about a helicopter crash in Calabasas. Then I saw his name.

Kobe Bryant.

My heart stopped. I felt this immediate, visceral punch to my stomach. I remember standing up so quickly that my coffee mug tipped over, spilling across the counter, but I didn't even care. I was frozen, staring at my phone in disbelief. The initial reports were confusing - was it true? Could this really be happening?

Then more details emerged. Not just Kobe, but his 13-year-old daughter Gianna was on the helicopter too. Other passengers. All gone. The numbness started to give way to a deep, aching sadness. This wasn't just a loss for basketball, but a devastating tragedy for a family, for his children.

I started crying - not the dramatic sobbing, but those quiet, shocked tears that come when something feels so fundamentally wrong with the world. Kobe had been more than just an athlete to so many of us. He was inspiration, determination personified. And now, suddenly, he was gone.

Overall Reflection

I overall think Claude does a good job of providing realistic instances in which a memory could occur, such as with social media and on the television. But I feel like the tone and level of description makes these instances not feel like a memory but instead like a narrative in a book, whereas I feel like an authentic memory where someone is trying to recall something be raw. They’re just trying to remember things on the spot and not worried about describing additional details.
